# A/B Testing — Winning Patterns

Evidence-backed patterns from conversion research. Use to generate initial hypotheses before running tests — these are not certainties, but strong priors worth validating for your specific audience.

---

## Headline Patterns

### Identity beats features for performance/aspiration brands

**Pattern:** "Built for [specific audience identity]" outperforms feature-list headlines for brands targeting achievement-oriented audiences.

**Evidence:** Identity-led headlines align with how the Hero and Achiever archetypes process decisions — they're choosing who to be, not comparing specifications.

**Example lift:** 18–25% increase in CTA click rate vs. product-feature headline in tested beverage/supplement brands.

**When it fails:** Technical audiences (Sage/Type 5) often respond better to proof-first. Identity feels manipulative to skeptical decision styles.

---

### Specific numbers beat round numbers

**Pattern:** "12,847 athletes" outperforms "12,000+ athletes" and "Thousands of athletes."

**Mechanism:** Specific numbers feel counted, not estimated. Round numbers signal marketing approximation and trigger distrust in high-Conscientiousness audiences.

**Exception:** For aspirational brands targeting Ruler/Achiever, specificity can feel "small" if the number isn't impressive. Only use specific numbers if they're large AND credible.

---

### The problem statement before the solution increases engagement

**Pattern:** Opening with an unresolved problem (the pain) before presenting the solution increases scroll depth and time on page.

**Mechanism:** Zeigarnik effect — unresolved tension creates forward motivation. The audience scrolls to find the resolution.

**Implementation:** Hero states the identity/outcome; the first content section (Section 2) states the problem explicitly.

## CTA Copy Patterns

### Risk reversal in microcopy outperforms feature statements

**Pattern:** Text under the button that removes risk ("60-day guarantee, no questions") consistently outperforms text that adds features ("All 3 flavors included").

**Evidence:** Buying anxiety peaks at the moment of CTA commitment. Risk removal at that exact moment reduces hesitation.

**Variants to test:**
- "60-day money-back guarantee" — strong for skeptical audiences
- "Cancel anytime" — strong for subscription models
- "Free shipping over $50" — strong for first-time buyers

---

### First-person CTA copy ("I want to...") tests variably

**Pattern:** "I'm ready to perform" vs "Start Performing" — first-person consistently wins for identity-driven audiences (Achiever, Hero) but loses for analytical audiences (Sage, Type 5 — who find it presumptuous).

**Rule:** Test when primary persona is Hero/Achiever. Skip when Sage/Thinker.

---

### Action specificity vs. commitment level

**Pattern:** Cold traffic converts better on low-commitment CTAs ("See how it works") than high-commitment CTAs ("Buy now").

**Calibration:**
| Traffic temperature | CTA copy direction |
|--------------------|-------------------|
| Cold (first visit, ads) | Low commitment — "Learn more", "See the science" |
| Warm (organic, referral) | Medium — "Try it free", "Start your first month" |
| Hot (return visit, email) | High commitment — "Add to cart", "Subscribe" |

## Social Proof Patterns

### Proof that's adjacent to the CTA converts better than proof in a standalone section

**Pattern:** One testimonial immediately above or below the primary CTA outperforms the same testimonial in a dedicated testimonials section.

**Mechanism:** Proof closest to the decision moment reduces hesitation. Testimonial sections are processed as "marketing content" and often skimmed.

**Implementation:** Pull one high-impact testimonial into the hero section or repeat it directly above the CTA.

---

### Reviewer specificity increases credibility

**Pattern:** "Marcus R., 3× marathon finisher, Portland OR" outperforms "Marcus R., athlete."

**What adds credibility:**
- Achievement (marathon finisher, state champion, years of experience)
- Location (real, specific)
- Verified badge (third-party — Amazon, Google Reviews)

**What undermines credibility:**
- Job titles from unrelated fields ("Software engineer who loves running" — off-brief)
- Photos that look like stock photography
- Quotes that sound like marketing copy

---

### NSF Certified / third-party certification near skeptic objections

**Pattern:** For supplement/nutrition brands, displaying NSF Certified (or similar third-party) next to ingredient claims dramatically reduces skepticism among high-Conscientiousness audiences.

**Why:** Third-party means the brand has external accountability. This is the top trust signal for informed consumers who read labels.

## Pricing & Friction Patterns

### Subscription framing as "cost per day" reduces perceived commitment

**Pattern:** "$1.85/day" outperforms "$55/month" for subscription products. Daily framing aligns with daily product usage.

**When it backfires:** Premium pricing brands — "per day" framing can make a luxury product feel commodified.

---

### Anchoring with a higher price increases conversion on the target tier

**Pattern:** Showing a premium option ($99/mo) before the target ($49/mo) makes the target feel accessible. The high anchor creates a reference point.

**Classic pattern:** Three-tier pricing where the middle tier is what you actually want people to choose.

---

## FAQ & Objection Patterns

### FAQ questions in the audience's exact language outperform marketing language

**Pattern:** "Is this safe if I'm on medication?" outperforms "Are there any contraindications?"

**How to find the right language:** Audience research Phase 2 (audience-research skill) — pull exact phrases from Reddit, Amazon reviews, forums.

---

### Answering the objection you're afraid of increases trust

**Pattern:** "Why is this more expensive than Gatorade?" addressed directly with a full, honest answer consistently increases conversion among skeptical audiences vs. ignoring the question entirely.

**Mechanism:** Addressing the hard question signals confidence. Brands that avoid their obvious objection look like they're hiding something.

## Persona-Calibrated Test Direction

| Persona | Start testing here | Expected lift |
|---------|--------------------|--------------|
| Achiever / Hero | Identity-first headline | High (15–25%) |
| Sage / Type 5 | Specific proof numbers in headline | Medium (8–15%) |
| Lover / Experiencer | Outcome-first headline, lifestyle photography | Medium (10–20%) |
| Explorer | Problem-first headline, challenge framing | Variable |
| Ruler | Status signals, exclusivity language | High if right (20%+) |

---

## What Rarely Works (Low PIE Tests)

| Test | Why it underperforms |
|------|---------------------|
| Button color | Most pages already have sufficient contrast; marginal |
| Image swaps (similar content) | Copy and message drive behavior more than photography |
| "Limited offer" fake urgency | Informed audiences distrust it; damages brand |
| Form field reordering | Marginal vs. removing fields entirely |
| Hero video vs. image | Video often reduces LCP; net negative on many devices |

---

## Sources and Evidence Base

These patterns derive from:
- Conversion research from CXL Institute (Peep Laja, et al.)
- Unbounce conversion benchmark reports
- Nielsen Norman Group UX research on trust and credibility
- Applied: WiderFunnel, Optimizely documented case studies
- Domain: Sports nutrition, supplement, DTC consumer brands specifically

All patterns are starting hypotheses — validate against your specific audience with sufficient sample size before treating as rules.
